⚛ Development of Atomic Theory

~400 BC Greek Philosophers
The Philosophical Atom · Democritus & Leucippus
Democritus proposed all matter is made of tiny, indivisible particles called atomos (🌀 — “uncuttable”). Matter was thought to be built from four elements: earth, fire, water, and air. These ideas were purely philosophical — no experimental evidence existed.
💡 These ideas persisted for ~2000 years until experimental chemistry began identifying real, distinct elements — inspiring Boyle's work.

1661 Robert Boyle
Chemical Elements · The Sceptical Chymist
Boyle proposed that some substances cannot be broken down into simpler ones — these are the true chemical elements. He rejected the Greek four-element model and established an empirical, experimental approach to chemistry.
💡 Boyle’s concept of elements directly inspired Dalton to propose that each element is made of a unique type of indivisible atom.

1803 John Dalton
The Billiard Ball Model · Atomic Theory
Each element is made of identical, indivisible solid spheres. Atoms of different elements have different masses. Compounds form when atoms combine in fixed whole-number ratios. Atoms cannot be created, destroyed, or subdivided.
💡 Becquerel’s discovery of radioactivity (1896) showed particles could escape from inside atoms — proving atoms are NOT indivisible as Dalton claimed.

1896 Henri Becquerel
Discovery of Radioactivity
Becquerel discovered that uranium spontaneously emitted energetic radiation. This proved that particles originate from within the atom itself — shattering Dalton’s idea of the atom as an indivisible whole. The following year, J.J. Thomson discovered the electron.
💡 If radiation came from inside atoms, atoms must have internal structure. This triggered a search for sub-atomic particles — leading directly to Thomson’s electron discovery.

1897 J.J. Thomson
The Plum Pudding Model · Cathode Ray Experiments
Thomson discovered the electron using cathode ray tubes — a negatively charged particle ~1840× lighter than the atom. He proposed the Plum Pudding Model: a diffuse sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded throughout, like plums in a pudding.
💡 Rutherford devised the Gold Foil Experiment to test Thomson’s model. The results were so unexpected that Rutherford said it was “as if you fired a shell at tissue paper and it bounced back”.

1911 Ernest Rutherford
Gold Foil Experiment → The Nuclear Model
Rutherford fired alpha particles at thin gold foil. Most passed straight through, but a small number deflected at large angles — some even bounced directly back. This proved the atom contains a tiny, dense, positively-charged nucleus at its centre, with electrons orbiting at a great distance (atom is mostly empty space).
💡 Problem: classical physics said orbiting electrons should continuously radiate energy and spiral into the nucleus. Bohr solved this by quantising electron energy levels.
▼ click to expand
1913 Niels Bohr
The Bohr Model · Quantised Electron Shells
Bohr proposed electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed energy levels (shells) numbered n=1, 2, 3… Electrons can only jump between shells by absorbing or emitting a photon of specific energy (hν). This explained atomic emission spectra. Shells hold 2, 8, 18… electrons (formula: 2n²). This was the beginning of quantum theory.
💡 The Bohr model couldn’t explain multi-electron atoms or fine spectral lines. Schrödinger’s quantum mechanics (1926) replaced fixed orbits with probability clouds. Chadwick’s neutron discovery (1932) also completed the nuclear picture.
▼ click to expand
1932 James Chadwick
Discovery of the Neutron · The Complete Nuclear Atom
Chadwick bombarded beryllium with alpha particles and detected a new neutral particle — the neutron — with mass similar to the proton. This completed the modern nuclear model: a nucleus containing protons (+1) and neutrons (0), surrounded by electrons (−1) in shells. Protons and neutrons together are called nucleons.
💡 With the neutron discovered, Schrödinger’s quantum mechanical model added mathematical precision — describing electrons not as particles in fixed orbits but as probability clouds in s, p, d, f sub-shells.

1926 – Today Schrödinger & Quantum Model
s, p, d, f Orbitals — Probability Clouds
Schrödinger’s equation describes electrons as probability clouds (atomic orbitals) rather than particles in defined orbits. Electrons occupy sub-shells: s (spherical, max 2e⁻), p (dumbbell, max 6e⁻), d (4-lobed, max 10e⁻), f (max 14e⁻). Three rules: (1) lowest energy fills first; (2) orbitals of equal energy fill singly before pairing (Hund’s rule); (3) max 2 electrons per orbital (Pauli exclusion). This is the model used throughout A-Level Chemistry.
✅ This quantum model explains ionisation energies, periodic trends, chemical bonding, and molecular geometry — the complete foundation of modern chemistry.
